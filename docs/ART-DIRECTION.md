**Honest framing:** The real tension is not "stylized vs. real" but "highest fidelity vs. everything else you've locked" — and your own docs already break the tie before you pick up a brush. RESEARCH.md flags max-fidelity browser WebGPU as RISKY and states the LOVED quality is "mood/reactive audio, not polygon count" (line 36); DESIGN.md §11 locks a comedic Lethal-Company funny-scary tone with a full contextual-physics suite (zero-G, decompression, gore debris) that puts dozens of dynamic objects on screen; and PLATFORM-AND-QUALITY caps the free browser build at HIGH under a ~180MB first-load budget while reserving ULTRA (SSGI) for the paid desktop client. Every one of those facts pushes away from photoreal and toward a controlled register where lighting, post, physics-density and audio carry the "wow" — because in a browser the quality ceiling is set by frame stability and art coherence, not triangle count. The honest reinterpretation: "highest 3D quality in a browser" should mean highest ART quality (stylized != low quality, and it ages better), not highest fidelity you can't sustain at 60fps with the physics suite running. The evidence leans toward a stylized-realistic base with a one-notch-more-grounded monster, but this is a lean to pressure-test on a concept board, not a decision to make from prose — generate the register row first and let the same corridor, seen four ways, settle it.

### Recommendation (a lean, NOT a lock — decide from the board)

RECOMMENDATION (an exploration lean, NOT a lock): adopt STYLIZED-REALISTIC as the base register, executed as the MIXED hybrid — a grounded, readable, cheap-to-populate ship world plus THE CHORUS pushed one notch more grounded (tighter materials, subsurface flesh, sharper silhouette) under the same flashlight, post stack and AgX grade. One line why: it's the only register that scores High on every constraint that actually binds this project at once — the locked funny-scary tone, the browser VRAM/180MB download budget for full-physics chaos, a defensible 'best-looking browser co-op horror' marketing claim, small-team feasibility (reuses the whole specced M-LOOK pipeline), dismemberment/debris legibility in dark+fog, AND a real Low->Ultra fidelity ladder for the paid Steam upsell — while the hybrid's tonal contrast is the mechanism that lets the dark CHORUS lore and the funny moment-to-moment play both be true and fixes the enemy-readability risk already flagged in RESEARCH.md. Still: generate the register row before you commit — let the same corridor, four ways, confirm it.

### Top register × direction combos to render FIRST


**Stylized-realistic x IRON LUNG + grounded CHORUS (the hybrid spine) — LEAD**
- _Look & feel:_ Reads as real wet industrial steel at a glance, but materials are slightly simplified, edges chunkier, grime hand-placed for readability rather than photoscanned chaos — Dead-Space-remake lighting on Lethal-Company-legible geometry (cf. - _Why it fits:_ Lowest production risk — the entire specced M-LOOK pipeline (baked lightmaps, trim sheets, wet-floor SSR, emissive discipline) already aims here, and ART-DIRECTION calls Iron Lung register-agnostic and the safe spine. Grime hides budget sins so you can afford the dense dynamic-object count the physics-comedy suite needs; a teammate yeeted across a decompressing room reads as funny BECAUSE the grounded-but-readable surface keeps it legible. The grounded monster directly fixes the RESEARCH.md line-54 readability risk. Most likely to pass the M-LOOK 'that looks AAA' bar AND ship.

**Cel-shaded / comic x LEVIATHAN BLOOM (living comic book) — DIFFERENTIATOR SWING**
- _Look & feel:_ Ink-outlined biomechanical horror, flat-ish bioluminescent color zones with hard rim light, halftone/screen-tone grime in shadow — the pulsing fleshy ship as a living EC-Comics splash page (Hi-Fi Rush / Sludge Life meeting Scorn). The most original, most clip-able identity in the deck — the trailer that doesn't look like every other space-horror game. **Low-poly / PS1-retro x IRON LUNG (Lethal-Company-grim-funny) — THE FORCING FUNCTION**

Generate it specifically to FORCE the explicit decision the project keeps deferring: keep the 'highest 3D quality in a browser' AAA ambition, or adopt the actual look of your references? **Stylized-realistic x STERILE WOUND (clean canvas, maximum splatter) — CONTRAST ZONE, not whole-game**
- _Look & feel:_ Clinical matte-white Apple-store surfaces (cheap to texture — almost no grime authoring), then violated by wet black bio-resin and oxygenated-blood spray. The horror is contrast; the comedy is that white floors make every physics-comedy mess maximally visible — a friendly-fire incident paints the whole room and everyone sees the carnage.

Blood/gore/debris pops hardest against white = peak clip material; clean rooms are technically easier (less near-black baked-lightmap dependence) and give the Director huge light->dark dynamic range. But it fights the locked near-black/flashlight-is-everything core loop if it's everything — so it earns a board frame to test the splatter-contrast idea, not a full 5-shot board in round one.

### Register risks

- Picking a register from prose instead of the board. The strongest de-risk is the Stage-0 register row; choosing now (even the lead recommendation) before seeing the same corridor four ways forfeits the cheapest, highest-leverage decision artifact you have.
- Chasing photoreal for the headline. It structurally competes with the locked physics suite for the same browser VRAM and the ~180MB first-load budget, blows the small-team art budget, ages worst, and fights the comedic tone — it loses on five of seven criteria and 'wins' only on the one thing (raw fidelity) the browser can't sustain at 60fps.
- The Ultra-upsell trap on flat styles. If you fall for low-poly or pure cel, the paid Steam ULTRA tier (SSGI, 4K pack) looks the same as the free browser build — there's almost no fidelity ladder, undercutting the premium product. Stylized-realistic is the register that scales credibly without breaking the browser build.
- Hybrid gap becoming a chasm. If the grounded CHORUS uses a different lighting model / post stack / grade than the stylized world, it looks pasted in from another game. Enforce: more detail in material and silhouette only, lit by the same flashlight, graded by the same AgX, outlined by the same pass — and test it explicitly in the board.
- Cel/low-poly reading juvenile against the dark LORE. The locked lore (THE CHORUS, 'restoring comms is the monster's win') must stay dark; a flat/cartoon register only works if the writing and ElevenLabs audio hold the dread — verify the tonal contract before committing to a comic register.
- Genre saturation on pure low-poly. Lethal Company / Content Warning / the HauntedPS1 scene have made this look ubiquitous; even if tone-perfect, it offers the least visual distinctiveness and the weakest premium claim.
- Stylized-realistic reading as derivative Dead Space. The lead candidate's main aesthetic risk (per ART-DIRECTION Iron Lung cons) is 'we've seen this' — it needs a signature ship silhouette + livery system to own the look, or it blends into the genre's industrial default.
- Over-generating permutations. Producing all 16 register x direction combos burns the M-ART budget; discipline is the ~6-tile register row plus 2-3 finalist boards only, with Sterile Wound / Analog Ghost held as per-zone blends.

## The four directions (the *direction* axis — explore within the chosen register)


### Direction A — IRON LUNG (Industrial-Grime Dead-Space)

- **Vibe:** Heavy, oppressive, claustrophobic blue-collar deep-space industry that has rotted. The ship feels like a foundry someone left to die: thick steel, exposed machinery, rust weeping from every seam. The horror is attrition — the place is trying to kill you slowly through neglect, dark, and cold before any monster does. Player feeling: dread of being crushed/forgotten inside a giant dead machine. This is the safest bet because it's exactly what the locked M-LOOK/06-rendering-mood docs already aim at (near-black corridors, wet metal, scorch, failing fluorescents).
- **References:** Dead Space 1/2 (the Ishimura — religious-industrial mining hauler), Alien (Nostromo refinery decks), The Thing (Carpenter, grime + dread), GTFO (cold metal complex + darkness as enemy), artist refs: H.R. Giger (restrained, for the alien only), Maciej Kuciara / Ben Mauro industrial-hull concept work.
- **Palette:** Gunmetal #2B2F36, cold steel #3A4048, rust-oxide #6E4A33, hazard-amber emissive #E8A33D, sodium-vamp practical #C97B3B, sickly fluorescent cyan #9FD0FF, near-black void #05070A, dried-blood decal #4A0C0C.
- **Materials:** Brushed/painted steel with heavy roughness break-up, exposed riveted plate, corroded pipe runs, oil-stained grated floors (wet/puddle mask for SSR), foam-and-rubber gaskets, scorched composite panels, stencilled hazard livery, frost on cold sections. Trim-sheet driven (matches the locked trim-sheet plan).
- **Lighting:** Practical-motivated: dying sodium worklights (warm amber) fighting failing fluorescents (cold cyan), most of the ship pitch-black so the flashlight is the only reliable source. Hard pools of light with long black gaps. Emissive hazard strips and engine glow are the only saturated colour. Exterior: single warm distant sun key + cold nebula rim, hull mostly in deep shadow.
- **Creature aesthetic:** Necromorph-adjacent: re-purposed human/industrial biomass — bone, tendon and torn flesh fused with ship metal and cabling. Reads as 'the crew became part of the machine.' Dismemberment-friendly (clear limb segmentation), wet sinew catches the flashlight. Apex (Maw boss) is a hull-scale fusion of bodies and bulkhead.
- **Pros:** Lowest production risk — entire existing pipeline (baked lightmaps, trim sheets, wet-floor SSR, emissive discipline, flashlight-as-hero) is already specced for it. Greeble-dense kitbash/Quaternius assets read instantly. Grime hides low-poly/texture sins, so it looks AAA cheaply. Dismemberment design and 'light = safety AND attracts apex' mechanic land perfectly. Most universally legible 'space horror'.
- **Cons:** Most familiar/derivative — risks 'we've seen this' if not pushed with a strong signature silhouette. Brown-grey palette can read muddy if grade isn't disciplined. Hardest to look distinctive against Dead Space itself; needs a unique ship silhouette + livery system to own it.

### Direction B — ANALOG GHOST (Retro-Futurist Alien-Isolation)

- **Vibe:** A 1970s-imagined future that aged badly: CRT monitors, chunky beige plastic, amber phosphor readouts, magnetic tape, rocker switches. Warm, tactile, lived-in and eerily quiet — the horror is loneliness and a single unkillable presence, not a swarm. The COMMS-RESTORE climax sings here because the whole fiction is analog comms gear. Player feeling: trapped in a haunted, humming machine from a future that never happened.
- **References:** Alien: Isolation (Sevastopol — built directly from 1979 Alien production design), Alien (1979) Nostromo bridge, Solaris (1972), Moon (2009), 2001: A Space Odyssey (HAL-era warmth), Ron Cobb / Syd Mead production design, Ridley Scott's 'used future'.
- **Palette:** Beige plastic #C9BBA0, nicotine off-white #DAD2BE, amber CRT phosphor #FFB000, oxblood trim #5C2A2A, avocado-panel green #6F7A4B, teak/brown control surfaces #4A3826, deep analog black #0B0C0E, indicator red #D7352B.
- **Materials:** Injection-moulded beige plastic with scuffs, brushed aluminium trim, padded vinyl seating, fabric-wrapped cabling, dot-matrix labels, glass CRT bezels with scanlines/curvature, magnetic-tape reels, illuminated rocker switches, yellowed acrylic. Warmer roughness, less wet metal than A.
- **Lighting:** Warm tungsten and amber phosphor glow; light is more present than in A but sickly and unstable. CRT flicker as ambient light source, emergency reds, long humming corridors lit by failing strip lights. Exterior: hull is plated, riveted, antenna-bristling — backlit by a cold star so the analog running-lights read as tiny warm dots.
- **Creature aesthetic:** One apex Xenomorph-style biomechanical hunter — sleek, black, wet, elegant, mostly unseen; the dread is its absence. Less gore, more silhouette and sound. Other enemies are rare and equally restrained. Suits the 'sound-based detection + single apex stalker' design beautifully; dismemberment is downplayed in favour of being hunted.
- **Pros:** Strongest atmosphere-per-dollar and most distinctive identity. Warm analog palette is a refreshing contrast to the genre's blue-grey default and photographs beautifully. CRT/tape/comms fiction directly powers the COMMS-RESTORE climax and diegetic HUD. Emissive CRT glow is cheap and gorgeous. Audio-fear Director shines when the ship is quiet.
- **Cons:** Tension model leans single-stalker, which slightly fights the 6-archetype/swarm/L4D-director plan — needs reconciling (analog ship, modern threat). Beige/brown can look drab without strong amber accents. Bespoke retro props (CRTs, switches, tape decks) are more custom-modelled, less kitbashable than industrial greebles.

### Direction C — STERILE WOUND (Clean-White Prometheus Body-Horror)

- **Vibe:** Pristine, expensive, almost Apple-store corporate cleanliness — and then something organic and wrong contaminates it. The horror is contrast: pure white surfaces smeared with black bio-fluid and blood, beautiful light made nauseating. A high-end research/medical vessel where the experiment got out. Player feeling: violated calm — a clinical sanctuary turned charnel house.
- **References:** Prometheus / Alien: Covenant (Weyland labs, the medpod), The Cabin in the Woods (clean lab + monsters), Annihilation (the Shimmer — iridescent organic bloom), Ex Machina (clean architecture menace), Dead Space: medical/sprawl decks, artist refs: Steve Messing, Dariusz Zabrocki clean-sci-fi interiors.
- **Palette:** Clinical white #EEF1F4, cool grey #C2C9D0, glacier-cyan glow #7FE0E6, surgical chrome #D9DEE3, bio-black contaminant #0E0B12, oxygenated blood #8A1220, iridescent infection sheen #A06CE0/#46D9B0, warning amber #F2B33A.
- **Materials:** Seamless matte-white composite panels, polished chrome, frosted backlit glass, ceramic medical surfaces, brushed white alloy — then overlaid with wet black bio-resin, blood pooling, fungal/iridescent growth creeping along seams. Strong clean-vs-organic material contrast is the whole look. SSR on glossy floors is spectacular here.
- **Lighting:** Bright, even, soft-box clinical light (the opposite of A/B) that the Director can corrupt — kill banks of lights to plunge clean rooms into black, strobe surgical lamps, let emergency red flood white walls. The brightness makes darkness scarier by contrast. Exterior: a sleek pale-hulled science vessel, smooth and elegant, lit hard by a clinical star.
- **Creature aesthetic:** Body-horror: glistening black-bio organisms, deformed crew, parasitic growths, Prometheus-style 'wet birth' creatures. Iridescent oil-on-water sheen. Dismemberment reads vividly against white floors (blood spray is the visual payoff). Highly photogenic gore.
- **Pros:** Maximum visual contrast and shock value; blood/horror pops hardest against white. Genuinely distinct from the genre's dark-industrial default — memorable. Brightly lit scenes are technically EASIER (less reliance on the near-black baked-lightmap mastery) and give the Director huge dynamic range (light→dark is the scare). Clean surfaces are cheap to texture (less grime authoring).
- **Cons:** Bright clean rooms fight the locked 'near-black corridor, flashlight-is-everything' core loop and the scarce-battery/light-attracts-apex mechanic — the whole game can't be bright, so this works best as a CONTRAST ZONE (e.g. the med-bay/lab wing) rather than the whole ship. Clean surfaces show low-poly/aliasing flaws mercilessly. Risk of reading 'cold tech demo' not 'horror' if the organic contamination isn't strong.

### Direction D — LEVIATHAN BLOOM (Bio-Organic Alien-Infested Wetness)

- **Vibe:** The ship is no longer a ship — it's a host. An alien biomass has colonised the hull: pulsing membranes, resin webbing, sphincter doors, fleshy tunnels grown over the original steel. Hot, wet, breathing, fungal. The horror is being inside a living thing's gut. Player feeling: visceral revulsion + awe; you are food walking through a digestive tract.
- **References:** Alien (the Derelict / egg chamber + Giger's biomechanical hive), The Thing (assimilation), Dead Space 3 / The Callisto Protocol (biomass overgrowth), Scorn (Giger/Beksiński bio-architecture), Annihilation (mutation bloom), Half-Life (Xen/headcrab infestation), artist refs: H.R. Giger, Zdzisław Beksiński, Plamen 'PoroBG' Petkov.
- **Palette:** Resin-black #14100E, fleshy mauve #5A2E3A, bile-green bioluminescence #6FE07A, membrane red #7A1B22, chitin grey-violet #3B3340, spore-cyan glow #4FD4E0, fat-yellow ichor #C7B25A, wet sheen highlight #E8D7C0.
- **Materials:** Translucent subsurface-scatter membranes, slick wet resin (heavy SSR/clearcoat), chitinous ribbing, pulsating sacs (vertex animation), fungal mats, sinew cabling fused to original ship steel showing through in patches. The 'original ship vs infestation' material contrast tells the story of the colonisation.
- **Lighting:** Bioluminescence is the light source — sickly green/cyan pulses from organic growths breathing in the dark, plus the flashlight. Wet surfaces throw the light everywhere. Director can make the bioluminescence pulse with tension (the ship 'reacting' to the player). Exterior: a captured ship visibly encrusted/overgrown, organic tendrils breaking the hull silhouette, eerie glow leaking from breaches.
- **Creature aesthetic:** Pure Giger biomechanical: everything is the creature — walls, doors, enemies are all one organism. Wall-Crawlers, Spitters, Swarmers grown from the biomass; the Maw boss is the ship's 'heart/mouth.' Dismemberment exposes glowing innards. The strongest fit for the full 6-archetype roster as a unified ecosystem.
- **Pros:** Most viscerally scary and the most ORIGINAL silhouette — instantly not-Dead-Space. Bioluminescence is a gorgeous, cheap, Director-controllable light source that animates the world. Wet SSR/subsurface look is a WebGPU showpiece. Unifies all 6 enemies into one coherent ecology and makes the ship itself feel alive/hostile (serves priority 7: context-aware behaviour — the ship reacts).
- **Cons:** Highest art-production cost and risk: organic membranes need subsurface scattering, vertex animation, custom sculpted assets — far less kitbashable than industrial kits, so M-LOOK's 'prove it cheap' promise is hardest here. Can tip into gross-out fatigue or read 'busy/noisy.' Pure-organic loses the relatable 'human ship' grounding; best deployed as an ESCALATION (clean ship early → biomass-consumed later) rather than from frame one.

## Concept-art pipeline (how we produce the examples)

## Concept-Art Pipeline — producing the frames the user will judge (2026 tooling)

The goal of M-ART is **decision-grade concept frames in days, not weeks** — enough to choose a style, not final art. Everything below is "explore fast, lock a look, hand the chosen look to 3D look-dev." Per 2026 studio practice, the value is in *controlling* generation (reference-first, style anchors, paintover), not raw generation — AI never produces shippable assets, only the design target ([Inkration](https://inkration.com/ai-art-tools-for-game-studios-in-2026-what-actually-works-for-production/), [Deconstructor of Fun](https://www.deconstructoroffun.com/blog/2026/2/9/designing-games-in-the-age-of-ai)).

### Tool stack (and what each is for)
- **Midjourney v7 — primary mood/atmosphere exploration.** Best-in-class art-directed cinematic output, deliberate color grading and composition ([AI Video Bootcamp](https://aivideobootcamp.com/blog/midjourney-complete-guide-2026/), [AI Flow Review](https://aiflowreview.com/midjourney-v7-review/)). This is where the 4 directions are born and where the "wow" frames come from.
- **FLUX (FLUX.1 / FLUX.2 Pro, incl. Krea-tuned) — precision + photoreal + consistency experiments.** Open, controllable, strong at brand/technical consistency and photoreal surfaces; FLUX.1 Krea specifically "breaks the AI look" for grounded realism ([302.AI](https://medium.com/@302.AI/flux-1-krea-test-how-it-breaks-the-ai-look-in-image-generation-cfd5a049b634), [Flowith](https://flowith.io/blog/flux-2-pro-vs-midjourney-v7-commercial-artists-full-control/)). Use for the technical/orthographic prop and material plates, and image-to-image style locking.
- **Krea — real-time iterative refinement.** Restrained, rational palette and live param tuning ([302.AI](https://medium.com/@302.AI/flux-1-krea-test-how-it-breaks-the-ai-look-in-image-generation-cfd5a049b634)) — good for dialing a frame live with the user in the room during the review.
- **Magnific (now Freepik suite) — Creative-mode upscale + detail enrichment.** Adds believable texture/greeble detail at 4K–10K, with a dedicated 3D/digital-art mode; explicitly used for refining game textures/3D-asset detail ([Magnific](https://www.magnific.com/blog/understanding-freepiks-upscaler-modes/), [Magnific](https://www.magnific.com/ai/image-upscaler)). Run final board frames through this so they read as finished concept paintings.
- **Human paintover (Photoshop/Krita)** — composition fix, scale-cue insertion (capsule, running lights), color-grade correction. Non-negotiable per 2026 practice; the AI output is a base, not the deliverable ([iXie](https://ixiegaming.com/traditional-vs-ai-enhanced-art-workflow/), [Sunstrike](https://sunstrikestudios.com/en/blog/ai-in-game-art/)).

### Getting a CONSISTENT style across frames (the hard part)
Long-term consistency does NOT come from better prompts — it comes from anchors and reference re-feeding ([Sunstrike](https://sunstrikestudios.com/en/blog/ai-in-game-art/), [MindStudio](https://www.mindstudio.ai/blog/ai-image-video-gaming-concept-art-trailers)). Per direction:
1. **Lock a Style Reference per direction.** In Midjourney v7, generate 6–10 exploration frames, pick the strongest, and either reuse it as `--sref <image>` or grab a numeric **`--sref` code** from MJ's style library to pin the look across every subsequent frame; tune influence with **`--sw`** (0–1000, default 100 — push to ~250–400 to hold a strong derelict mood) ([MJ Style Reference docs](https://docs.midjourney.com/hc/en-us/articles/32180011136653-Style-Reference)).
2. **Lock the creature/hero prop with `--oref` (Omni-Reference).** Once a creature or signature prop (e.g. the broken comms array) is approved, carry it across the docking-bay/corridor/command-centre frames via `--oref` for 90%+ subject consistency, while `--sref` holds the lighting/mood ([MJ Omni Reference docs](https://docs.midjourney.com/hc/en-us/articles/36285124473997-Omni-Reference)).
3. **Fix the prompt skeleton + a 3–5 token style lexicon per direction.** e.g. Direction A: `["wet gunmetal", "sodium amber worklight", "failing fluorescent", "industrial greeble", "near-black"]`. Reuse verbatim every frame; never introduce a competing style cue mid-series ([Flowith](https://flowith.io/blog/midjourney-v7-vs-flux-artistic-style/)).
4. **Hold composition/seed.** Keep a fixed seed and aspect for the matched shot-list frames so the 4 directions are compared at the same camera, then image-to-image (FLUX/Krea) the approved MJ frame to bring stragglers into line.
5. **Re-feed approved art.** Once direction X's exterior is approved, feed it back as the style ref for X's interiors — the canon image, not a prompt, is the source of truth.

### Prompt structure (one template, swap the lexicon per direction)
`[shot/camera] of [subject] — derelict space-horror, [3–5 style lexicon tokens for the direction], [palette named-hex cues], [lighting language], [material cues], cinematic, volumetric, film grain, anamorphic --ar 21:9 --sref <dirCode> --sw 300 [--oref <heroRef>]`

### Shot list (the SAME 5 frames in all 4 directions = a comparable board of 20)
1. **Exterior hero** — tiny capsule foreground, km-scale derelict hull, Earth + cold sun, nebula rim, emissive window-grid running-lights (21:9, the make-or-break scale frame).
2. **Docking bay** — capsule docking into a dark cavernous bay; first interior reveal; scale jump human→ship.
3. **Corridor** — the core loop frame: flashlight-as-hero, near-black, one failing panel, wet floor, a story decal/corpse.
4. **Command Centre** — the COMMS-RESTORE climax room: the comms/CRT array, escalation, the hero set-piece.
5. **A creature** — the archetype that defines the direction's body-horror language, full-body for dismemberment legibility.
(Optional 6th per direction: a **material/trim plate** — orthographic surface swatch generated in FLUX — that becomes the literal texture target for 3D.)

### How a chosen style flows into 3D look-dev + assets
1. **Approved board → look-dev target sheet.** The chosen direction's 5 frames + palette hexes + material plates become the pinned target board taped next to the M-LOOK build (the docs already call for offline mood targets).
2. **Materials.** Extract the palette + trim language into the trim-sheet authoring (Poly Haven/ambientCG bases graded to the hexes) so the real-time surfaces match the concept.
3. **Hero ship + props.** Kitbash the exterior from **Kitbash3D 'Spaceships'** (perpetual, kitbashed/baked) toward the approved silhouette; corridor base from **Quaternius MegaKit (CC0)**; for bespoke creatures/props the concept can drive **Meshy-6 / Tripo v3.1** image-to-3D as a blockout, then retopo + detail in Blender (license-clean, you own the output). Magnific-upscaled concept frames double as texture/detail reference for the sculpt.
4. **Grade match.** The AgX/ACES post grade + PostUniforms (exposure, saturation, fog, vignette) in `06-rendering-mood.md` are tuned in M-LOOK until the live frame matches the concept frame side-by-side — that match IS the M-LOOK GREEN bar.

### Cost/legal note
All concept frames are **internal decision artifacts only** — never shipped in the browser GLB. Shipped assets remain CC0 / Kitbash3D-perpetual-baked / per-item-verified per the existing LICENSES discipline. Keep generated concept art out of the redistributed build to avoid any model-output licensing ambiguity.

## The M-ART milestone

## M-ART — Art-Direction Decision Gate (the NEW first milestone, BEFORE M-LOOK)

**One-line:** Before any real-time 3D capital is spent, produce a board of concept frames in 4 distinct art directions so the user can SEE the ship inside and out and DECIDE on a style (or a blend). It is cheap (days), purely 2D/AI concept work, and it decision-gates the entire visual build.

### Why it comes first
The locked roadmap starts at M-LOOK (build the look in real-time 3D). But M-LOOK already assumes a chosen look (cold industrial near-black). The user's #1 priority is "see the art FIRST, with examples, and choose." M-ART inserts that choice **before** M-LOOK so M-LOOK builds the *chosen* style in 3D instead of pre-assuming it. Sequence becomes: **M-ART (choose the look, 2D) → M-LOOK (prove the chosen look in real-time 3D, GREEN/RED) → M0 netcode → …**

### Scope (deliberately tiny)
- **IN:** 4 art directions × the 5-frame shot list = a **~20-frame concept board** (exterior hero, docking bay, corridor, command centre, creature), each direction internally style-consistent via `--sref`/`--oref`; a one-page palette+material+lighting spec per direction (the four StructuredOutput directions above); 2–3 paintover-polished + Magnific-upscaled "hero" frames per direction for the side-by-side.
- **OUT:** zero 3D, zero engine, zero code, zero netcode/audio/ECS. No real-time anything. No final assets. This is 2D concept exploration only.

### Deliverable
A single review board (e.g. a Figma/PDF/contact-sheet) showing the 4 directions side-by-side at matched shots, plus the 4 written direction specs. The user reviews it cold and **picks one direction, or a documented blend** (e.g. "Direction B analog warmth for the lived-in decks, escalating into Direction D bioluminescent biomass toward the command centre" — blends are explicitly allowed and encouraged since 'ships are the maps' lets different ships/zones carry different looks).

### Acceptance bar — "have we chosen a look?"
- [ ] All 4 directions are rendered at the **same 5 shots** so they're genuinely comparable (not cherry-picked angles).
- [ ] Each direction is **internally consistent** (one `--sref` mood across its 5 frames; the creature carried via `--oref`).
- [ ] Each frame sells the brief: km-scale exterior, dark interior where flashlight-is-hero, a readable creature, the COMMS-RESTORE command centre.
- [ ] Every direction has a one-page spec: named palette hexes, key materials, lighting language, creature aesthetic, pros/cons, player fantasy.
- [ ] At least 2 hero frames per direction are paintover-corrected + Magnific-upscaled so they read as finished concept paintings, not raw gens.
- [ ] **DECISION RECORDED:** the user picks a direction or a named blend, written down as the canonical look brief that M-LOOK builds toward. This is the gate — M-LOOK does not start until this line is checked.

### Time/cost
Days, not weeks (target ~3–5 days of generation + paintover). One person with Midjourney v7 + FLUX/Krea + Magnific + Photoshop. Near-zero asset spend (no kit purchases needed yet — those wait for M-LOOK once the look is chosen). This is the cheapest possible de-risk of the single most important creative question.

### How M-ART hands off to M-LOOK
The chosen direction's 5 frames + palette hexes + material plates become the **look-dev target board** pinned beside the M-LOOK build. M-LOOK's existing GREEN bar ("a first-time viewer says 'that looks AAA'") gains a second clause: **"and it matches the chosen M-ART concept board side-by-side."** The chosen palette feeds the PostUniforms grade and trim-sheet authoring directly. M-LOOK then builds exterior + 1 interior of the *chosen* style; on GREEN, its renderer code promotes into `packages/engine` for M1 exactly as already specced.

## Open questions

- TONE COMMITMENT: Which of the 4 player fantasies does the user actually want — crushing industrial attrition (A), lonely analog hunted (B), clinical body-horror shock (C), or visceral living-ship infestation (D)? They serve different fears and the audio Director, enemy roster, and core 'light-is-safety' loop are tuned differently for each.
- BLEND vs PURE: Because 'ships are the maps', do we want ONE locked look for the whole campaign, or a per-ship/per-zone escalation blend (e.g. A or B early ships → C/D contamination later)? Ship 1 (Cargo Hauler) most naturally reads as Direction A or B; a blend changes the M-ART board into 'pick the arc', not just 'pick one frame'.
- 6-ENEMY ROSTER FIT: Directions B (single apex stalker) and C (clinical) partly fight the locked 6-archetype + L4D-swarm-director plan, while A and D embrace it. Does the enemy design flex to the chosen look, or does the look need to serve the fixed roster? This should be resolved before the creature frames are finalized.
- CORE-LOOP vs BRIGHTNESS: Direction C (and parts of B/D) include bright/lit spaces that tension against the locked 'near-black corridor, scarce battery, flashlight-attracts-apex' loop. Confirm whether bright zones are allowed as contrast set-pieces or whether the whole game must stay near-black (which favors A/D).
- COMMAND-CENTRE / COMMS FICTION: The COMMS-RESTORE climax is in every ship. Should the comms tech be analog/CRT (pushes Direction B's identity hard) or sleek-holographic (pushes C) or bio-corrupted (D)? This single prop drives a lot of the diegetic HUD and command-centre concept frame.
- PRODUCTION-RISK TOLERANCE: Direction D (bio-organic) is the most original and scary but the most expensive/least kitbashable, putting the M-LOOK 'prove it cheap in days' promise most at risk. Is the user willing to spend more 3D look-dev time for D's originality, or prioritize the low-risk A/B path to a fast GREEN?
- WHO PAINTS OVER: 2026 practice requires human paintover/art-direction on every concept frame — is there an artist (or is the user doing it), and is a Midjourney + FLUX/Krea + Magnific toolchain already licensed/budgeted for M-ART?
