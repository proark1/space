# SIGNAL LOST — Story Bible (LORE)

_The narrative spine that pulls players in. Delivered diegetically (audio logs, the ship AI, comms, environment) — no cutscene dumps. Compiled 2026-06-27. Needs a narrative-design owner before Ship 1 content authoring._


## The hooks (why people keep playing)

- THE TITLE IS A TRAP: 'restore comms' is the game's signature heroic act AND the monster's victory condition — to call Earth for help is to widen the channel that lets the thing out. The win button is the monster's win button.
- THE SIGNAL THAT SHOULDN'T EXIST: every beacon in the dead fleet is off, yet one signal is still transmitting OUT. In the first 60 seconds VESTA says 'Correction — one signal active. It is not on our manifest.' That single line is the whole campaign's pull.
- THE MIMIC REVEAL (Ship 4 twist): the 'distress signal' you've chased ship-to-ship is the enemy mimicking a human cry for help, baiting rescuers in to carry it home. You are not the rescue crew. You are the delivery.
- THE ENEMY IS ONE THING, MADE OF SOUND: not a roster of aliens but a single colony resonance — the CHORUS — that learns by listening and answers by mimicry, growing the 6 enemy archetypes as tuned 'acoustic roles' (the Stalker is its hunting voice, the Swarmer its throat, the Maw its mouth). Lore and the sound-based detection mechanic are the same idea.
- THE FLEET SILENCED ITSELF ON PURPOSE: the crews smashed their own transmitters and welded bulkheads from inside to keep it from getting out. The commander's last order, found corrupted on the final ship: 'Do not restore comms. Whatever asks you to — do not answer it.' And you've been turning them back on.
- YOUR COMPANION'S VOICE TURNS: late-game the Chorus has learned VESTA's voice (and your dead crew's, and your own broadcast). A line that sounds like your friendly AI telling you to restore comms might not be. ElevenLabs Voice Design weaponized as the 'almost-right voice' instrument.
- THE FINAL CHOICE reclaims the title: at the deep-space master relay, restore it and the signal reaches Earth (finishing the fleet's nightmare) — or overload it and let the SIGNAL stay LOST. The game's name stops being a problem to fix and becomes the only safe ending.

## Story bible

# SIGNAL LOST — Story Bible v1

> Co-op space horror. 1–4 players. The signal that was lost is the only thread you have — and pulling it drags you deeper into a dead fleet.
> This bible is engineered to the locked plan: 6 enemy archetypes, 4 ship archetypes, capsule transits as the campaign spine, COMMS-RESTORE in every command centre, and an all-diegetic ElevenLabs delivery layer. No cutscene dumps.

---

## 1. The One-Line Premise

**A deep-space mining fleet went silent mid-transmission. You are the salvage-and-rescue crew sent to recover the black boxes — and the thing that silenced the fleet is using the silence to hunt.**

The central, memorable question — the thing every player should still be chewing on after the credits:

> **The distress signal never stopped. So who — or what — is still transmitting?**

Everything ladders to that. The crews are dead. The beacons are dead. The comms are dead. And yet, faint under the static of every ship, there is a signal *going out*. You spend the campaign restoring transmitters to call Earth for help. The horror payoff is realizing the enemy wants the transmitters restored too — it is learning to *speak*, and you are teaching it how to reach home.

---

## 2. The Hook — The First 60 Seconds

The opening is the Earth-launch on-rails capsule sequence (reuse of existing tech). Lore is delivered entirely through comms chatter and the HUD while the player is strapped in and cannot do anything but listen — the most captive audience in the run.

**Beat-by-beat (target ~60s, before pointer-lock control is granted):**

1. **0–8s — Black, then a voice.** Mission-control AID voice ("VESTA," our friendly station AI) over capsule radio: calm, clinical, reading a manifest. *"Capsule integrity nominal. Salvage authority granted. You are cleared for the Kepler-Aldrin graveyard. Recover the fleet logs. Bring them home."* Establishes job (salvage/rescue), stakes (recovery), tone (corporate-cold, not heroic).
2. **8–25s — The launch.** Earth falls away. Window glare resolves into the dark. VESTA reads the loss: *"Fleet KESTREL went dark four hundred days ago. Eleven ships. Zero survivors recovered. Zero distress beacons active."* A beat. *"Correction. One signal active."* The first wrongness.
3. **25–45s — The signal plays.** A garbled audio fragment auto-decrypts on the HUD — a crewman's last log, ElevenLabs-degraded into radio-rot. You catch maybe four words clean: *"…it's not coming from outside…"* Then it loops. It should not be looping. VESTA, fractionally less calm: *"Source of transmission: unknown. It is not on our manifest."*
4. **45–60s — The dock.** The derelict fills the window — a Star-Wars-scale dead hull, running lights out, one window deep inside it lit and flickering. The capsule slams the airlock. Hiss. Dark. VESTA, quiet now: *"Comms to Earth are down past the relay shadow. Restore a transmitter and I can hear you again. Until then… you're on your own."* Flashlight clicks on. Control granted.

The hook lands four things in under a minute without a single cutscene: **who you are** (salvage/rescue), **what's wrong** (a signal that shouldn't exist), **the core loop** (restore comms), and **the dread** (something is already inside, and it's transmitting).

## 3. What Actually Happened — The Truth

> Tiered for the team: players never get this whole; it assembles from fragments across ships. Keep the full truth as the writers' reference.

**The fleet.** The KESTREL fleet was a deep-system **mining and survey convoy** for a resource-extraction concern (working name: **Aldrin-Vesta Salvage & Survey**, "AVS"). Eleven ships, ~1,400 souls, on a multi-year tour of a far asteroid belt and a derelict alien object found drifting there — designated **Object KESTREL-0**, the reason the fleet was really out there. The cover job was ore. The real job was the Object.

**The discovery (the dreadful one).** KESTREL-0 was not debris. It was a **dormant lattice** — a non-biological, sound-structured organism that survives as **resonance**: it has no fixed body, it propagates through any medium that can carry vibration (hull metal, air, water lines, *radio*). For however long it drifted dead-cold and silent, it was inert. The survey crew brought it aboard the research vessel, warmed it, and — fatally — **scanned it with active sonar and broadcast pings.** They gave it sound. They woke it up.

**What the enemy is.** It is not "aliens" plural — it is **one distributed thing**, a *colony resonance* the surviving logs call **the CHORUS**. It cannot think the way we do; it **learns by listening and answers by mimicry.** The "monsters" players fight are not separate species — they are **bodies the Chorus grows out of the dead** and out of the ship's own biomass and materials, each tuned to a different *acoustic role*:

- **Stalker** — the hunter-listener. Grown to track sound; it learned to go silent to corner prey. The Chorus's predation voice.
- **Swarmer / Hive** — the throat. Cheap, many, vocal — they are how the Chorus floods a space with noise to mask the Stalker and overwhelm.
- **Brute** — the wall. Grown from heavy bodies to break sealed doors and force prey out of safe quiet rooms.
- **Wall-Crawler / Ambusher** — the reach. It learned the ship has a *third* dimension humans forget to watch.
- **Spitter** — the range. It learned that distance is safety and took it away, lobbing acoustic-corrosive matter.
- **The Maw** (boss) — the Chorus's **mouth**: a command-node body it grows near each ship's command centre, because that is where the transmitter is — and the Chorus wants the transmitter.

**What it wants.** It wants what every signal wants: **to be received.** Drifting dead in the belt, it was a message with no listener. The fleet gave it a voice (sound) and a medium (a ship full of radios). Now it is trying to **transmit itself** — to reach a larger listener. Earth is the larger listener. Every time the crew restored comms to call for help, they widened the channel home. The fleet didn't go dark by accident. **The fleet went dark on purpose — the last sane crews destroyed their own transmitters to keep the Chorus from getting out.** And you, the rescue crew, are flying ship to ship *turning them back on.*

**The fate of the crew.** Logs reveal it in stages: first the survey team's wonder, then the auditory hallucinations (the Chorus learning their voices and calling them in their friends' tones), then quarantine, then the realization that it spreads through the comms relays between ships, then the fleet-wide self-silencing — crews smashing their own transmitters and welding bulkheads from the inside. The very last act of the fleet's commander was a standing order, found half-corrupted in the final ship: **"Do not restore comms. Whatever asks you to — do not answer it."**

The twist that recontextualizes the whole campaign: **the "distress signal" pulling you ship to ship is the Chorus, mimicking a human cry for help, baiting rescuers in to carry it home.** You are not the rescuers. You are the delivery.

## 4. The Players — Who Goes In, and Why

**They are an AVS salvage-and-recovery crew** — blue-collar deep-space contractors, not soldiers. Think long-haul wreck divers: competent, underpaid, here for the recovery bonus on a lost fleet's black boxes (and, officially, "any survivors"). This framing earns the genre's beloved tonal range: they swear, they panic, they crack jokes over comms, and the scarce-ammo/scarce-battery economy reads as *we are not equipped for this* rather than *we are a strike team.* It also justifies why four randos with mining tools are aboard a horror ship.

**Their stake.** Money first (the recovery contract), then survival (the relay shadow cuts them off — the only way home is forward, restoring transmitters), then dawning horror (they realize what restoring comms is actually doing). The arc of motivation across the campaign: **paid → trapped → complicit → trying to undo it.**

**Solo vs co-op, in fiction:**
- **4 players** = a full recovery crew, voices on the same channel. Proximity voice chat *is* the fiction — the Chorus hears you, so talking is both your lifeline and your liability ("too scared to speak"). This is the intended texture.
- **1–2 players / mic-less** = framed as a crew that's already been whittled down, or a lone "tether operator." VESTA, the station AI, fills the empty channel with mission-control voice (the solo "mercy profile" already in the design) so a solo player is never in silence-without-character. Mic-less players get VESTA-relayed pings and the non-voice COMMS-RESTORE fallback so the climax still lands.

**The crew is deliberately under-characterized as fixed individuals** — players bring their own friend-group identity. Characterization lives in the *world* (the dead crew's logs are richly written), not in scripted protagonist banter that would fight the players' own voice chat.

## 5. The Multi-Ship Arc — Ships Are The Maps

The campaign is a one-way descent through the **KESTREL graveyard**, ship to ship, each clear "leeching" the next ship's location from the restored transmitter (matches the locked capsule-transit fiction). Each ship answers one more piece of the central question, and each COMMS-RESTORE climbs the ladder from *call for help* to *the terrible thing restoring comms does.*

**The escalation of the COMMS-RESTORE climax** is the campaign's spine:
- It starts as **hope** (call Earth, broadcast our living voices home — the original viral beat).
- It curdles into **doubt** (the logs warning *don't restore comms*).
- It ends as **the choice** (restore the master relay and you might save yourselves — or finish what the fleet died to prevent).

### The five-ship arc

| # | Ship (archetype) | Story role | COMMS-RESTORE beat | What the player learns |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Cargo Hauler** *(industrial)* | The way in. A working ship, recently dead. Proves the fun; establishes the Chorus as a *presence*, not yet understood. | Restore **this ship's short-range transmitter** → broadcast the crew's live terrified voices to Earth as proof-of-life. Pure hope. (The locked M6 climax.) | Something killed this crew fast and it hunts by sound. First Stalker, first vent swarm. |
| **2** | **Research Vessel** *(labs/cryo)* | The "what went wrong here" ship. The origin ship — this is where KESTREL-0 was brought aboard, scanned, and woke. | Restore a **relay** to re-light the next ship's beacon — but the logs here first say *don't.* Hope, with the first crack. | The enemy is **one thing** that learns by listening; it came from the Object; *the crew woke it with sound.* |
| **3** | **Military Cruiser** *(armored, hostile AI)* | The fleet tried to fight and contain it here. Quarantine failed. An active, paranoid ship AI is barricaded in and may help or hinder. | Restore a **hardened command relay** through a hostile-AI lockout — the AI begs you not to. | The Chorus spreads **through the comms relays themselves.** Restoring comms is how it travels. The dread reframes the whole loop. |
| **4** | **Civilian Liner / Evac Ship** *(the human tragedy)* | The softest, most populated ship — where the fleet tried to *evacuate.* It became a slaughter-funnel. The emotional gut-punch ship. | The transmitter is intact but **someone already restored it** — and what's broadcasting in human voices is the Chorus, learning Earth's reply. | The "distress signal" you've been chasing **is the Chorus mimicking a cry for help**, baiting rescuers. *You are the delivery.* The full twist lands here. |
| **5** | **Alien-Infested Wreck** *(the source / final)* | The deepest, darkest, most transformed ship — biomass over the hull, the Chorus's heart, the **deep-space master relay.** Final confrontation. | The master relay. **Restore it and the signal reaches Earth** — finishing the fleet's nightmare — *unless* the crew finds the commander's last option: broadcast the Chorus *back into the Object* / overload the relay to silence it for good. The final crescendo and the campaign's real choice. | The commander's order, the way to end it, and the cost. The campaign's thesis pays off. |

**Why this works as escalation:** every ship is a fully satisfying self-contained restore (one crescendo each, per the locked rule), but the *meaning* of "restore comms" inverts across the five — from salvation to damnation to a final, earned choice. The marketing hook (broadcast your live voices home) is intact on Ship 1; by Ship 4 the player understands the horror of having done it.

### Ship archetypes & their fixed story roles
(Maps onto the four locked biomes plus the Liner as the human-tragedy ship; build order 1→2→3→(4)→5 as content cadence.)
- **Industrial (Hauler):** *the threshold* — normalcy, then violation.
- **Research/Clinical (Vessel):** *the origin* — where it woke; cold curiosity punished.
- **Military (Cruiser):** *the failed war* — containment, the hostile-AI ally/antagonist, the relay-spread reveal.
- **Civilian (Liner):** *the human cost + the twist* — the evac that became a funnel; the mimic-signal reveal.
- **Organic Wreck:** *the source + the choice* — the Chorus's body; the master relay; the ending.

Post-launch content slots cleanly: any new ship is a new chapter of the graveyard between existing beats ("the Medical Frigate," "the Mining Platform"), each adding one more dead-crew story and one more restore-variant, without touching the five-beat spine.

## 6. Diegetic Delivery — Lore Without Slowing The Horror

**Rule: the player never stops moving for story.** No cutscene dumps, no codex walls you must read. Lore is *optional depth that rewards the curious and never gates the scared.* Five channels, all tied to the ElevenLabs pipeline:

### 6.1 VESTA — the station AI (your channel)
A pre-baked + optional-Flash ship/mission-control voice (the existing ship-AI bark path). VESTA is the player's companion, tone-setter, and the safe-room narrator. She delivers the *current* mystery in measured doses ("I've decrypted another log fragment — playing now"), runs the solo mercy-voice profile, and is the emotional anchor whose calm erodes across the campaign. Crucially, **late game VESTA becomes unreliable** — the Chorus has learned her voice. A line that sounds like VESTA telling you to *restore comms* might not be VESTA. (ElevenLabs Voice Design makes the "almost-VESTA" mimic a deliberate, terrifying instrument.)

### 6.2 Audio logs — the dead crew (the deep lore, opt-in)
Scattered recorders, datapads, intercom backups. **Found, not forced** — picking one up plays it over your HUD while you keep walking/fighting; it ducks under combat audio and resumes after (never on a scare's critical path). These carry the *human* story: the survey team's wonder, the hallucinations, the quarantine, the self-silencing. ElevenLabs gives every dead crewmember a distinct designed voice; the **horror move is the Chorus replaying those same voices** later as bait. Collecting logs feeds the meta-lore unlock (granted on ship-clear) — exploration reward without exploration tax.

### 6.3 Comms chatter — the fleet's last hours
Intercepted inter-ship radio, looping distress fragments, the *wrong* signal bleeding through. Often the first thing you hear entering a zone (degraded, ElevenLabs radio-rot processing). This is where the central mystery breathes — including the recurring, slowly-clarifying **"distress signal"** that the player eventually realizes is the Chorus mimicking a human cry.

### 6.4 Environmental storytelling — corpses & scenes (the wordless lore)
The richest, cheapest channel. Staged tableaux the player reads at a glance, no text:
- A barricaded room welded *from the inside*, the transmitter inside smashed by its own crew (the self-silencing — seen before it's explained).
- A wall of bodies at an evac door that opened the wrong way (the Liner).
- A research bay with the Object's empty cradle, sonar rigs still pointed at it (the origin, on the Vessel).
- Chorus biomass growing *out of and along* the ship's speaker grilles, wiring, and comm relays — visually selling "it lives in the sound system" before any log says so.
- Crew who clearly walked *toward* a voice — frozen mid-step, facing a speaker.

### 6.5 The broadcast-back-to-Earth motif (the throughline & the climax)
Every COMMS-RESTORE is the same ritual escalated: power the transmitter, hold the command centre, and **your own live voices go out.** Early ships make this triumphant (proof-of-life, the viral beat). Later ships make it *horrifying* — because by then you know what you're widening. The motif is the single recurring diegetic device that carries the central question from hope to dread to the final choice, and it's the one beat anchored to **real human voices** (proximity voice chat), which inoculates the AI-voice risk exactly as the design intends.

**Pacing protection:** all five channels obey the silence budget. Logs/comms duck and yield to the Scare Director; nothing story-related ever talks over a stinger or fires on a scare's critical path. Lore *fills the lulls* — it is literally engineered to live in the authored quiet windows between spikes, giving the forced silence a *reason to listen* and turning "the quiet" into the place the story lives.

## 7. Tone & Themes

**Tone:** cold, industrial, lonely, occasionally darkly funny (the blue-collar crew framing earns the co-op comedy tail the design wants). Dread over gore, though the dismemberment delivers the spike. Reverent of the Dead Space / Alien-Isolation *language* of light, sound, and dread — but built on an original organism and an original myth (a sound-based colony resonance, not a parasite/xenomorph), so it stands clear of those IPs.

**Core themes:**
1. **Sound as the medium of horror** — the monster *is* sound; the gameplay (sound-based detection, the audio-fear Director, proximity voice) and the lore are the same idea. The thing you use to coordinate and survive is the thing that hunts you.
2. **Communication as contagion** — to call for help is to spread the thing. The act of *reaching out* is the act of *letting it out.* Comms-restore, the game's signature, is also its moral horror.
3. **The unreliable familiar voice** — your companion AI, your dead friends, your own broadcast turned against you. The most comforting sounds become the most dangerous.
4. **Complicity** — you are not the heroes saving the fleet. You are the rescuers the trap was set for. The campaign asks whether you finish the call or cut the line.

**The memorable twist (the thing they tell their friends):** *The distress signal was never a survivor. It was the monster, mimicking a cry for help, to make rescuers carry it home — and "restore comms," the heroic thing you do every single ship, is exactly how it spreads. The game's win button is the monster's win button. The final choice is whether to answer the call or finally let the signal stay lost.*

That last line reclaims the title: **SIGNAL LOST** stops being a problem to fix and becomes the only safe ending.

## Open questions

- Final-ship ending shape: is the 'restore vs overload the master relay' choice a true branching ending (two outcomes, replay value, NG+ framing) or a fixed authored climax with one canonical resolution? Branching adds VO/scene cost but deepens the thesis.
- Fixed protagonists vs blank-slate crew: the bible deliberately under-characterizes the players so friend-groups bring their own identity. Confirm we want zero scripted protagonist banter (lore lives in the dead crew), or whether 1–2 light recurring crew voices are wanted for solo/marketing.
- Naming/branding lock: confirm 'the CHORUS' (the enemy), 'VESTA' (station AI), 'KESTREL fleet', 'Object KESTREL-0', and 'AVS / Aldrin-Vesta Salvage' as canon, or substitute. These thread through every audio log and the Audio Forge prompt library.
- Ship 4 (Civilian Liner / Evac) is a 5th biome beyond the 4 locked archetypes (industrial/research/military/wreck). Either (a) add the Liner as a distinct biome, or (b) fold the human-tragedy twist into the Liner-as-Cruiser-variant or into the Wreck. Affects art-content volume vs the 4-kit plan.
- How explicit should the 'you are the delivery / win button = monster's win button' twist be made, and when? Risk: revealed too early it can make players feel their core action is futile (a known co-op feel-bad). Recommend the full reveal at Ship 4, with only dread-seeds before — needs playtest validation against the pacing telemetry.
- Mic-less / solo framing of the COMMS-RESTORE climax: the non-voice fallback must still deliver the broadcast-home emotional beat. Decide the diegetic substitute (VESTA relays a text/telemetry burst? pre-recorded 'proof-of-life' ping?) so the signature moment isn't voice-gated.
- Tone calibration of the comedy tail: the blue-collar-salvager framing invites co-op levity (friendly-fire, light-as-bait panic). Confirm how much comedic room the writing should leave vs. relentless dread, and whether VESTA's voice carries any of it.
